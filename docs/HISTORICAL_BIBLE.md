# Historical Bible

## 1. Historical scope

The game is set in Moscow from the evening of Wednesday, 21 August 1991, to the morning of Thursday, 22 August 1991, during the collapse of the attempted coup by the State Committee on the State of Emergency, commonly abbreviated as GKChP.

The attempted coup began after hard-line officials moved against Mikhail Gorbachev and the reform process. A proposed New Union Treaty, scheduled for signing on 20 August, would have decentralized substantial authority to the Soviet republics and was an important precipitating factor. The coup leaders announced that Gorbachev was unable to perform his duties, restricted media, and held him at his dacha in Foros. Resistance centered visibly around the Russian White House under Boris Yeltsin. By 21 August, military support was failing and the coup was collapsing. Gorbachev returned to Moscow early on 22 August.

This frame provides the correct dramatic tension: the outcome is becoming clear, but information remains fragmented, delayed, partisan, and emotionally difficult to accept.

## 2. Terminology rules

Use terminology according to speaker and context.

### Neutral production language

- August 1991 coup attempt
- attempted coup
- GKChP
- State Committee on the State of Emergency
- coup leaders or plotters
- collapse of the coup

### Character language may include

- putsch;
- coup;
- restoration;
- emergency committee;
- constitutional order;
- counter-revolution;
- reactionaries;
- democrats;
- traitors;
- patriots;
- “the committee”;
- “those eight.”

A character's vocabulary signals position and may change over the night. The narrator and interface should not adopt euphemistic language without attribution.

Do not describe the coup as a “counter-coup” in neutral production text unless quoting a character. Historically, it was an attempted seizure of power by hard-line officials against Gorbachev's government and reforms.

## 3. Public-event spine

The exact event sequence must be verified in the historical ledger before final scripting. The following is a dramatic planning spine, not a substitute for source checking.

### Prior context

- 18 August: coup representatives confront Gorbachev at Foros and communications are cut.
- 19 August: GKChP announces emergency rule and Gorbachev's alleged incapacity; state television replaces much regular programming and `Swan Lake` becomes an enduring image of the coup; Yeltsin publicly resists from the White House.
- 20 August: the New Union Treaty had been scheduled for signing; barricades and public resistance continue; military loyalty is uncertain.
- early 21 August: three civilians are killed during a confrontation near a tunnel close to the White House.

### Game night

- 21 August evening: reports and rumors of troop withdrawal, defections, invalidation of GKChP authority, and collapse circulate.
- later 21 August: members or representatives of the coup leadership leave Moscow or attempt to reach Gorbachev in Crimea; accounts are confused.
- shortly after midnight, 22 August: Gorbachev departs Crimea for Moscow.
- around 02:00: his arrival at Vnukovo is televised or reported.
- early morning: arrests and searches begin; reports reach the public unevenly.

Do not stage every event as a clean live broadcast. The bar should receive a mixture of:

- official television;
- restored or competing Russian television;
- radio;
- telephone reports;
- street witnesses;
- technically inferred signal changes;
- false rumors;
- retrospective claims made only minutes later.

## 4. Broadcast policy

### Use fictionalized broadcast text

Write original brief bulletins inspired by sourced events. Do not reproduce long archival transcripts, announcer scripts, or copyrighted journalism.

### Signal and uncertainty

A broadcast can be:

- delayed;
- interrupted;
- distorted;
- contradicted by radio;
- visually clear but verbally vague;
- technically suspicious to Lev;
- interpreted differently by patrons.

### `Swan Lake`

The association of Soviet television and `Swan Lake` during the coup is historically central. Use it sparingly so it retains force.

- The underlying Tchaikovsky composition is public domain in many jurisdictions.
- A particular performance or recording may remain protected.
- Commission or create a rights-cleared original recording.
- Do not reproduce archival broadcast video.
- A short distorted fragment, television image of dancers, or patron recognition may be more effective than extended playback.

### Real political figures

Real figures may appear only through distant, stylized, or partially obscured broadcasts. Avoid making them fully interactive characters. Use original art derived from multiple historical references, not copied frames. Verify publicity, likeness, archival, and jurisdictional issues before release.

## 5. Fictional bar: Café-Bar “The Shift”

### Establishment type

The game should depict an aging **кафе-бар**, not a contemporary cocktail bar and not an elite hotel restaurant. It is a modest public establishment attached to or formerly administered through a district institution.

Working establishment history:

- opened in 1968;
- designed in late-Soviet modernist public-interior language;
- partial 1982 refurbishment introduced cheaper laminate, upholstery, fluorescent task lighting, and a color television;
- by 1991, repairs and substitutions have produced a layered interior;
- Galina protects the room from total decay despite supply difficulty;
- regulars have claimed particular seats for years.

This history should determine every material decision.

### Why a fictional site is preferable

- avoids falsely claiming exact knowledge of a real establishment;
- permits a plausible combination of sourced features;
- enables a room layout optimized for play;
- keeps public events factual while private lives remain clearly fictional.

The fiction must still be materially credible.

## 6. Environment accuracy checklist

### Architecture

Verify:

- ceiling height;
- wall finishes;
- door and window construction;
- public-building radiators and pipework;
- floor material;
- service counter dimensions;
- ventilation;
- lighting fixtures;
- coat storage;
- lavatory/service signage;
- electrical sockets and switches.

### Furniture

Verify or mark composite:

- pedestal tables;
- metal or wooden chairs;
- fixed or freestanding stools;
- counter materials;
- shelving;
- upholstery;
- ashtrays;
- service trays;
- menu holders.

### Electronics

Do not settle on a model from memory. Research and ledger:

- color television model and production period;
- radio receiver;
- telephone;
- cash register or till method;
- refrigerator or cooler;
- lighting controls;
- antenna and cabling.

A generic but period-correct device may be safer than a confidently wrong named model.

### Tableware and service objects

Research:

- faceted glasses and sizes;
- shot glasses;
- beer mugs;
- ceramic plates;
- cutlery;
- decanters;
- metal trays;
- tea-glass holders, if used;
- paper napkins;
- bottle closures;
- ashtrays;
- receipt and tab practices.

### Food and drink

Plausible categories include:

- vodka;
- beer;
- mineral water;
- tea;
- black bread;
- pickles;
- herring;
- sprats or simple open sandwiches;
- boiled egg;
- sausage or cheese when available.

Do not assign exact brands, bottle designs, serving sizes, or prices without a dated source. Shortage and availability should be specific to the fictional establishment rather than a universal claim about the USSR.

### Signage

All wording must be sourced or treated as fictional administrative text. Have Russian proofreaders review:

- opening hours;
- “no credit” or payment notices;
- sanitary-day notices;
- menu headings;
- staff-only signs;
- fire and evacuation notices;
- handwritten out-of-stock notes;
- television or telephone instructions.

Do not use pseudo-Cyrillic.

## 7. Avoiding anachronism

Reject or investigate:

- Russian tricolor displayed as settled national décor before the relevant decision and local adoption;
- post-Soviet coat of arms;
- post-1991 commercial brands or packaging;
- modern European beer taps;
- LED signs;
- flat-panel proportions;
- contemporary industrial-loft lighting;
- modern cocktail glassware;
- post-Soviet banknotes or price language;
- current fonts and signage systems;
- modern plastic chairs unless specifically sourced;
- pristine nostalgia-museum arrangements;
- universal abundance of Western cigarettes, liquor, or consumer goods;
- fashionable exposed brick and Edison bulbs;
- smartphones, digital clocks, modern switches, or current street furniture.

A faded Lenin image or official symbol may exist, but do not cover every surface with propaganda. Ordinary public interiors were not theme parks.

---

## 8. Social and cultural accuracy

### The Soviet Union was not only Russia

The cast should reflect a multi-republic state without treating non-Russian identity as decorative diversity. Timur's history, for example, must be researched with attention to Azerbaijan, mobility, language, family networks, and the violence and uncertainty of the late Soviet period.

Use specialist consultation for:

- Jewish Soviet experience and discrimination;
- military service in Czechoslovakia or Afghanistan where relevant;
- republic identity and nationalism;
- Party and workplace hierarchy;
- public catering and informal procurement;
- late-Soviet speech registers;
- age-specific slang;
- gender and workplace dynamics.

### Do not create ideological avatars

A patron who values Soviet social mobility may also remember repression. A critic may miss collective institutions. A Party official may have protected people. A victim may remain committed to communism. Complexity does not require false equivalence: documented state violence and discrimination still require clear treatment.

### Alcohol

Alcohol affects conversation but must not function as a universal key to “truth.” Some characters become performative, repetitive, quiet, ill, or more guarded. Service choices carry ethical and social consequences.

### Gender

Do not let the older men monopolize historical authority without challenge. Sasha and Galina have labor, family, and institutional memories of their own. Avoid using women only as caretakers, romantic interests, or off-screen casualties.

---

## 9. Language policy

### Source language

- Preserve Russian names, patronymics, and key institutional terms in research files.
- Dialogue may be authored in English first, but every line should be written as a translation of a plausible Russian utterance, not contemporary English internet speech.
- Avoid excessive “comrade” usage.
- Avoid making every character speak in aphorisms.
- Use patronymics and forms of address according to relationship and status.

### Localization

Recommended structure:

- source text IDs;
- English script;
- Russian adaptation by a native literary translator;
- historical and speech-register review;
- subtitle timing independent from voice files;
- glossary for untranslatable or historically specific terms;
- no text baked into art.

### Accent policy

For an English-language voice version, do not require broad simulated Russian accents. Either use Russian performances with subtitles or cast natural English voices and let vocabulary and rhythm carry the setting.

## 11. Source hierarchy

Prefer, in order:

1. dated primary documents, photographs, film, broadcasts, menus, catalogs, and eyewitness records;
2. reputable archives and museum collections;
3. peer-reviewed or specialist historical scholarship;
4. well-edited contemporary journalism and oral histories;
5. general reference works for orientation only;
6. social media and commercial image sites only as leads, never sole proof of a claim.

A photograph may prove that an object existed, but not that it was typical in every bar. Record the difference.

## 13. Consultant gates

Before the vertical slice is treated as historically locked, obtain review from at least:

- a historian or researcher of late Soviet political history;
- a material-culture or design researcher familiar with late-Soviet interiors;
- a native Russian language editor familiar with age and class registers;
- a cultural reviewer for any non-Russian Soviet identity given a principal role.

Review questions:

- What feels anachronistic?
- What feels stereotyped rather than observed?
- Which object or phrase would a person from the period immediately reject?
- Are institutional relationships plausible?
- Are patrons using knowledge they could not yet have?
- Does the game distinguish rumor from verified event?
- Does it accidentally make the coup's failure appear inevitable to people inside the night?

---

## 14. Historical acceptance gate

The slice passes only when:

- all public events in the slice have ledger entries;
- all visible exact models, signs, prices, insignia, and dated objects are sourced;
- no unresolved high-risk anachronism remains;
- a Russian language reviewer has checked signage and key dialogue;
- a historian has reviewed the event sequence;
- the bar reads as a lived establishment rather than a Soviet-themed set;
- uncertainty is represented as contemporary uncertainty, not writer confusion;
- fiction and public record are clearly distinguished.
