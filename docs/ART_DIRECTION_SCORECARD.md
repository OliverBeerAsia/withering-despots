# Art Direction Scorecard

## 1. Purpose

The visual-QA checklist prevents defects. This scorecard asks a harder question: **is the work memorable, specific, and emotionally useful, or merely competent?**

Do not assign a single numerical “beauty score.” Review each category as:

- `PASS — target quality`;
- `TARGETED EDIT — sound base, specific aesthetic weakness`;
- `REJECT — generic or incoherent direction`.

A key target may be approved only when every category passes. A technically clean but generic image does not pass.

---

## 2. Three-second read, thirty-second reward

At first glance, the viewer must understand:

- where the entrance is;
- where the patrons gather;
- where Sasha works;
- where the television and telephone are;
- which social group currently matters.

After thirty seconds, the image should reward attention with plausible evidence of use:

- a repair made with a different material;
- one chair replaced in another era;
- rubbed surfaces where hands actually touch;
- an unevenly stocked service area;
- object placement that reveals Galina's habits and the patrons' territorial claims.

Reject visual clutter that is noticed before the dramatic relationships.

At native 640×360, principal silhouettes, active props, and the current social focus must remain legible without labels or smoothing.

---

## 3. Composition and visual rhythm

Pass only if:

- the room has a clear large/medium/small shape hierarchy;
- negative space around heads and gestures is intentional;
- repeated tables, lamps, and chairs create rhythm without copy-paste regularity;
- foreground, middle ground, and rear depth read without photographic blur;
- asymmetry feels composed rather than accidental;
- the television is important without becoming the whole picture;
- the eye can circulate among patrons and return to Sasha;
- the scene has at least one memorable original compositional feature derived from the fictional establishment, not from a reference game's composition.
- large, medium, and small pixel clusters support the composition instead of adding uniform texture noise.

Warning signs of generic generated art:

- every wall equally decorated;
- all furniture facing the camera;
- centered symmetry with no dramatic reason;
- random objects filling every empty patch;
- detail density that is uniform from foreground to background;
- decorative foreground silhouettes hiding unresolved geometry.

---

## 4. Historical specificity without theme-park shorthand

Pass only if the room feels like **this** café-bar with a build and repair history, not an interchangeable “Soviet bar.”

Look for:

- material decisions linked to 1968 construction and 1982 refurbishment;
- ordinary administrative and service logic;
- a small number of sourced symbols rather than propaganda wallpaper;
- wear caused by work and regular use;
- furniture, glassware, electronics, and signage whose status is known in the ledger;
- details that could exist without serving a Western viewer's expectation of the USSR.

Reject:

- generic red-star saturation;
- piles of medals, busts, flags, and slogans added for instant recognition;
- universal gray misery;
- luxurious nostalgia styling;
- modern “industrial” décor disguised by muted color;
- random Cyrillic-like marks.

---

## 5. Material beauty

The scene should be attractive because materials are observed, not because every surface is glossy or elaborately textured.

Pass only if:

- varnish, painted plaster, laminate, vinyl/textile, metal, glass, ceramic, and paper remain distinct;
- edge wear and reflections follow material behavior;
- texture scale is coherent;
- local color survives lighting;
- the eye can distinguish clean, worn, repaired, and dirty without a global grime filter;
- repeated materials share a production language but not identical noise;
- glass, smoke, and television light remain restrained accents.

Reject:

- one photographic texture pasted over everything;
- oily highlights on matte objects;
- excessive micro-scratches visible at game scale;
- sepia used to manufacture age;
- painterly blur that hides construction errors;
- high-frequency generated detail that shimmers after scaling.
- a smooth painting passed through a pixel filter without manual cluster correction;
- isolated off-palette pixels, accidental dithering on faces, or material edges that crawl in motion.

---

## 6. Color and emotional light

Pass only if:

- the amber/cream/teal/brown base feels unified but not monochrome;
- controlled red and CRT cyan have narrative purpose;
- characters separate from the room by value and local contrast, not neon outlines;
- warm patron light and cooler work light describe social space;
- the room can progress from evening to dawn through overlays without losing its material identity;
- major public events visibly reweight the room without becoming music-video lighting;
- skin values remain believable in every lighting state.

A palette is not successful merely because its sampled colors match the swatches. Judge the distribution, hierarchy, and transitions.

---

## 7. Character distinctiveness and humanity

Every principal character must read as a particular person before dialogue.

Pass only if:

- silhouette, posture, head shape, clothing construction, and resting gesture are unique;
- age appears through structure and posture, not a generic wrinkle texture;
- caricature clarifies personality without dehumanizing the person;
- facial asymmetry is controlled and repeatable;
- clothing suggests maintenance, class, habit, and self-presentation;
- neutral and listening poses remain expressive;
- strong expressions do not break identity;
- all characters appear to inhabit the same drawn world without sharing one face template.
- each principal character remains identifiable in an unlabeled silhouette crop at native resolution.

Reject:

- interchangeable “old Russian man” faces;
- identical shoulder/belly/hand shapes;
- everyone scowling to signal seriousness;
- hyper-detailed faces on simplified bodies;
- young bodies with elderly face texture;
- costume shorthand standing in for characterization.

---

## 8. Pose and social staging

A dialogue game lives or dies through still poses.

Pass only if:

- the line of action supports the character's current social tactic;
- eye lines create believable groups and exclusions;
- hand placement communicates status, concealment, invitation, or withdrawal;
- seated weight is visible;
- bodies have personal space and do not look arranged for a class photograph;
- quiet listening poses are as intentional as speaking poses;
- one changed prop or posture can reframe a relationship without a new background.

Reject continuous generic gesturing. Stillness is part of the art direction.

---

## 9. Story density and restraint

Every prominent detail should do at least one job:

- establish period/material truth;
- support interaction;
- reveal maintenance or scarcity;
- reveal character habit;
- provide composition or lighting structure;
- change state during the night.

Remove details that only make the image “more Soviet,” “more cinematic,” or “more detailed.” Beauty here comes from selection.

The room should contain several stateful visual stories, for example:

- Arkady's document wallet moves from guarded lap to exposed table;
- Nikolai's medal case appears but remains closed;
- the bar ledger gains or loses a page;
- a repaired television antenna changes orientation;
- a chair is left empty after a departure;
- dawn reveals cleaning marks and abandoned objects.

---

## 10. Interface integration

The game is not a background-art gallery. Pass only if:

- subtitles and choices look native to the scene without pretending to be diegetic when they are not;
- interface ornament is restrained;
- focus states remain readable over all materials and light states;
- the cursor/action label does not destroy composition;
- the title and menu typography are original, proofread, and manually controlled;
- no 1990s reference is used to excuse small type, vague icons, or awkward verb controls.
- tactile press and selection feedback is original, immediate, and readable without copying another game's panel layout.

---

## 11. Candidate-selection order

When comparing generated or painted candidates, use this order:

1. structural fidelity;
2. historical plausibility;
3. compositional strength;
4. material and lighting coherence;
5. emotional atmosphere;
6. detail richness;
7. ease of correction and layering.

Never select a beautiful but structurally wrong candidate in the hope that geometry can be ignored later. Never select a geometrically correct but generic candidate without planning targeted art direction.

Choose one base. Do not splice unrelated faces, furniture styles, and rendering languages from many candidates unless each splice is consciously redrawn into the same system.

---

## 12. Aesthetic review questions

For every major target, answer with specific image regions:

1. What is the first thing the eye sees, and should it be?
2. What is the second visual beat?
3. Which empty area is doing useful work?
4. Which detail best proves this is a maintained late-Soviet public interior?
5. Which detail feels generic, decorative, or generated?
6. Where does shape repetition become monotonous?
7. Where do materials become muddy or indistinguishable?
8. Which character looks least integrated and why?
9. What can be removed to improve the image?
10. Which single targeted change would most improve authorship?

“Nothing needs changing” is not an acceptable first review of a key target.

---

## 13. Human approval checkpoint

Codex may prepare candidates, comparisons, corrections, and a recommended decision. A human art-direction checkpoint should approve:

- the empty-room target;
- the first canonical character;
- the first seated composite;
- the second character consistency test;
- the memory-vignette target;
- the full-cast composition;
- any new visual style or large deviation from the locked target.

This is not a concession that the pipeline failed. It is how taste, historical judgment, and character appeal remain intentional rather than accidental.
