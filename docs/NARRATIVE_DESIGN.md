# Narrative Design

## 1. Dramatic premise

The public event is fixed: the August coup fails. The private event is unstable: a group of regulars decide what their lives meant and what they will claim to have believed.

The central dramatic question is not “Who wins?” It is:

> What happens to a person's story when the institutions that justified it stop speaking with authority?

The player is not a judge with perfect evidence. Sasha is a witness with limited attention and relationships. The game should make the player care about truth without promising a complete truth.

---

## 2. Tone

Use a blend of:

- dry social comedy;
- historical unease;
- intimate confession;
- argumentative warmth;
- sudden danger;
- ordinary practical labor;
- grief that is usually understated.

Avoid:

- constant solemnity;
- broad slapstick;
- monologues that sound like textbook chapters;
- everyone being secretly noble;
- everyone being secretly monstrous;
- a Western observer who explains Russia to Russians;
- neat ideological reconciliation at dawn.

Humor should often precede or conceal pain. A patron may joke about queues, only for the exchange to reveal who could obtain medicine and who could not.

---

## 3. Point of view

### Sasha

Sasha is visible and authored, but the player defines her emphasis through choices.

Core traits that do not change:

- observant;
- practically competent behind the bar;
- remembers the regulars from earlier life;
- does not accept that age automatically grants authority;
- has enough distance to notice rehearsed stories;
- cannot safely say everything she thinks.

Player-defined strand selected in the opening:

1. **Film assistant** — notices edits, images, performance, and what a camera excludes.
2. **Interpreter** — notices vocabulary, foreign broadcasts, euphemism, and deliberate mistranslation.
3. **Technical editor** — notices procedural language, documents, dates, and contradictions.

Each strand unlocks approximately 5–8 unique questions across the full game. It must not create a superior route.

### Galina

Galina is a counterpoint to Sasha. She is neither an omniscient narrator nor a quest giver. She knows the regulars' habits and previous versions of their stories, but she withholds information for practical and personal reasons.

Her most important function is to puncture the old men's assumption that political history belongs only to officials, soldiers, and intellectuals.

---

## 4. Cast relationship map

Use relationships before ideology.

- Arkady once helped Gennady's family obtain an apartment, then later denied a request concerning Gennady's injured colleague.
- Lev repaired the bar's television and privately resents Arkady's role in blocking a promotion years earlier.
- Nikolai and Pavel share wartime reverence but disagree about state violence.
- Timur supplied scarce goods to Galina and knows which patrons benefited from informal access.
- Gennady calls Timur a profiteer while repeatedly relying on him.
- Pavel taught Sasha history and is disturbed when she remembers what he omitted.
- Yuri transported several patrons over the years and knows practical secrets without understanding their full context.
- Galina knows who paid, who borrowed, who disappeared, and who later claimed they had never visited.

The relationship map should create scenes in which a political claim immediately affects a personal debt.

## 5. Character dossiers

## 5.1 Arkady Sergeyevich Belov

**Age:** 72  
**Former role:** district Party committee official; later municipal administrative post  
**Silhouette:** narrow shoulders, long neck, carefully preserved suit, slightly forward head  
**Habit:** straightens papers and corrects terms  
**Prop:** leather document wallet and Party card  
**Speech:** complete sentences, procedural euphemism, avoids direct first-person blame

### Public belief

Institutions, however flawed, prevented chaos. Reform should have been orderly and centrally managed.

### Private fear

Without his title, he is merely an old man whose family advantages can be reinterpreted as corruption.

### Cherished achievement

He genuinely helped coordinate housing and heating during a difficult winter and remembers individuals whose lives improved.

### Compromised action

He signed a personnel decision that ended Lev's advancement after an unofficial warning about “reliability.” He tells himself he had no alternative.

### Early-night story

The committee may be clumsy, but an emergency is sometimes necessary.

### Late-night story

He recognized from the beginning that the committee lacked constitutional legitimacy.

### Truth unlocked by trust

He brought the Party card because he intended to display it if the coup succeeded and destroy it if it failed.

### Arc

Performance of authority → vocabulary adjustment → exposure → choice between preserving evidence and preserving dignity.

### Player leverage

- ask for exact responsibility rather than general history;
- offer or withhold a private place to destroy the card;
- repeat Lev's account;
- let Arkady finish his euphemism until it becomes absurd;
- return a favor without granting absolution.

---

## 5.2 Lev Borisovich Rubin

**Age:** 68  
**Former role:** broadcast and television engineer  
**Silhouette:** compact, slightly stooped, large glasses, rolled sleeves under old jacket  
**Habit:** tilts his head toward signal changes; taps a rhythm when thinking  
**Prop:** pocket radio, wire, small screwdriver  
**Speech:** clipped, technical metaphors, jokes as tests

### Public belief

No institution deserves trust when it controls the signal and hides the edit.

### Private fear

He has built his identity around seeing through deception, but he failed to confront discrimination when doing so might have cost his family more.

### Cherished achievement

He helped restore television transmission after a major technical failure and remains proud of public engineering.

### Compromised action

He allowed a colleague to take blame for equipment failure because he was already under scrutiny.

### Early-night story

The coup is technically incompetent and therefore doomed.

### Late-night story

Technical incompetence did not make the danger unreal, and his certainty was another form of vanity.

### Truth unlocked by patience

His daughter has sent documents for him to leave the country. He has not answered because leaving feels like confirming every accusation made against him.

### Arc

Detached analyst → challenged by deaths → personal implication → decision about departure.

### Player leverage

- ask him to repair reception, which may make him miss a human exchange;
- follow his technical inference over the official bulletin;
- challenge his certainty;
- protect his emigration letter from public exposure;
- ask what he did when his colleague was blamed.

---

## 5.3 Nikolai Fyodorovich Chernov

**Age:** 70  
**Former role:** army colonel, logistics and armored units  
**Silhouette:** broad chest softened by age, rigid neck, heavy hands, jacket kept buttoned too long  
**Habit:** aligns glass with table edge; stands for broadcasts  
**Prop:** closed medal case  
**Speech:** declarative, uses collective “we,” pauses before names

### Public belief

The army belongs to the state and people, not to political improvisers.

### Private fear

His service in Czechoslovakia in 1968 was not the reluctant stabilizing mission he has described to his family.

### Cherished achievement

He protected conscripts under his command and prevented a preventable convoy accident.

### Compromised action

He followed an order during the 1968 invasion that harmed civilians or enabled arrests. Keep the exact event researched and fictionalized; do not turn it into a sensational confession.

### Early-night story

Soldiers in Moscow will obey because discipline prevents bloodshed.

### Mid-night break

News of the tunnel deaths makes that claim impossible to sustain.

### Late-night story

An officer is responsible for what his soldiers do, even when orders come from above—unless he retreats into denial.

### Truth unlocked by silence

He has rehearsed a letter to the family of a Czech civilian for years and never sent it.

### Arc

Institutional pride → defensive anger → historical echo → responsibility or renewed denial.

### Player leverage

- serve tea instead of vodka when his hands shake;
- ask why the medal case remains closed;
- let Pavel invoke wartime sacrifice, then ask whether it applies to civilians;
- refuse to let him blame conscripts alone;
- offer paper for the unsent letter.

---

## 5.4 Gennady Mikhailovich Zorin

**Age:** 62  
**Former role:** skilled factory turner and union organizer  
**Silhouette:** thick forearms, workman's shoulders, loose formal shirt, forward lean  
**Habit:** slaps table for emphasis; touches an old finger injury  
**Prop:** folding ruler and factory photograph  
**Speech:** concrete, anecdotal, loud, repeats slogans sarcastically

### Public belief

Workers built the country while officials and intellectuals talked. Whatever replaces the system will still depend on people who make things.

### Private fear

The factory is already becoming irrelevant, and his authority there was partly borrowed from the same bureaucracy he mocks.

### Cherished achievement

He fought successfully for a colleague's housing and trained apprentices who became better workers than he was.

### Compromised action

He helped conceal a serious workplace accident to preserve the production plan and protect the shop from punishment. The injured worker never fully recovered.

### Early-night story

The coup is an argument among bosses. Ordinary people will outlast them.

### Late-night story

His own compromises were not separate from politics; they were how the system entered daily work.

### Truth unlocked by confrontation plus care

He visits the injured colleague but has never admitted his role.

### Arc

Mocking observer → champion of workers → exposed workplace power → practical act of restitution or retreat into resentment.

### Player leverage

- challenge his “ordinary people” category with Galina's labor;
- show the factory photograph to Arkady;
- ask what the production number concealed;
- refuse a third drink;
- let him repair a physical object in the bar, restoring pride without erasing guilt.

---

## 5.5 Timur Akhmedovich Mamedov

**Age:** 65  
**Former role:** public-catering procurement and supply manager  
**Silhouette:** rounded body, immaculate moustache, soft jacket, many keys  
**Habit:** notices stock levels; offers food before argument  
**Prop:** key ring, foreign lighter, address book  
**Speech:** stories built around people and exchanges; shifts languages or registers deliberately

### Public belief

Formal rules never described how the country actually functioned. Relationships, favors, and practical generosity kept institutions alive.

### Private fear

The networks that protected his family can become evidence of corruption or targets of ethnic suspicion. “The country” is splintering into places where relatives may no longer be safe.

### Cherished achievement

He kept kitchens supplied during shortages and quietly redirected food to a hospital.

### Compromised action

He participated in favoritism and excluded people without useful connections, while describing the system as mutual aid.

### Early-night story

Moscow men are arguing over a state that already feels different elsewhere.

### Late-night story

He does not know whether a looser union, independence, or restored central control would protect his family.

### Truth unlocked by discretion

A family member has disappeared or become unreachable amid conflict. Timur has been using procurement calls to search for news.

### Arc

Sociable mediator → outsider to Russian-centered argument → fear revealed → choice about staying, leaving, or asking for help.

### Player leverage

- let him use the telephone at the cost of missing a broadcast;
- challenge his romantic account of informal exchange;
- protect his address book;
- ask who never received his favors;
- serve food when others demand alcohol.

### Sensitivity requirement

His regional, ethnic, linguistic, and family history requires specialist consultation. Do not make him an exotic merchant stereotype or a symbolic “other.”

---

## 5.6 Pavel Ilyich Sokolov

**Age:** 76  
**Former role:** secondary-school history teacher  
**Silhouette:** tall but collapsed inward, long cardigan, ink-marked fingers  
**Habit:** corrects chronology, then loses his place  
**Prop:** old school notebook with amended lesson plans  
**Speech:** quotations, careful dates, sudden plainness when memory overwhelms theory

### Public belief

Communism remains a moral ideal distinct from the institutions that betrayed it.

### Private fear

The distinction has allowed him to avoid asking whether his teaching helped reproduce lies.

### Cherished achievement

He protected curious students, encouraged reading beyond the syllabus, and helped one family after a political dismissal.

### Compromised action

He omitted his own father's arrest from his lessons and publicly repeated official accounts he knew were false.

### Early-night story

The coup leaders have confused defense of socialism with defense of themselves.

### Late-night story

He must decide whether an ideal survives by refusing responsibility for its institutions.

### Truth unlocked by memory link

He once told Sasha not to ask about a blank space in a class photograph. He remembers exactly who had been removed.

### Arc

Moral distinction → student challenge → family memory → revised lesson or final refusal.

### Player leverage

- quote his old lesson back to him;
- ask for the missing name;
- compare his distinction to Arkady's procedural excuses;
- let him write in the ledger;
- return or keep a page he tears from the notebook.

---

## 5.7 Yuri Viktorovich Malakhov

**Age:** 51  
**Former/current role:** taxi driver, courier, fixer  
**Silhouette:** leather or synthetic jacket, quick lateral stance, younger energy than the room  
**Habit:** scans exits and evaluates objects  
**Prop:** plastic shopping bag, taxi keys, pager-like object only if verified and period-correct  
**Speech:** abbreviated, rumor-rich, practical, performs certainty

### Public belief

Rules are changing; the intelligent person moves before everyone else understands the new rules.

### Private fear

He has no stable institution, profession, or family protection. His confidence is survival theater.

### Cherished achievement

He has repeatedly helped people travel, obtain medicine, or move goods when formal systems failed.

### Compromised action

He exploits privileged information and has transported or sold goods without asking who was harmed upstream.

### Function

Yuri arrives after the room's social order is established. He carries outside sound, rumors, and the future's vocabulary. He must not become a cartoon proto-oligarch.

## 6. Chapter beat design

Each chapter needs:

- one public event;
- one practical bar problem;
- one relationship shift;
- one object-state change;
- one opportunity for silence;
- one claim that becomes unstable later;
- one visual change in the room;
- one optional scene missed by focusing elsewhere.
- an immediate visible response to the player's practical choice;
- a delayed cost, missed opportunity, or changed availability from a major choice.

Example for “The Dead at the Tunnel”:

- Public event: report of three civilian deaths.
- Practical problem: Gennady knocks a glass over during argument; Sasha can clean it immediately or continue listening.
- Relationship shift: Nikolai turns on Arkady for treating the army as a tool.
- Object change: medal case appears on table.
- Silence: after Nikolai says “They should not have been there,” waiting makes him correct himself.
- Unstable claim: Lev says the deaths will end the coup; later he admits he did not know.
- Visual change: television light drops, room sound narrows.
- Missable scene: Timur takes a telephone call while everyone watches Nikolai.

---

## 7. Attention-window design

An attention window is not presented as a game-show menu. The room changes and three hotspots become active.

Example:

- television sound rises;
- telephone rings behind Sasha;
- Arkady quietly asks Pavel for the notebook page.

The player may focus on one. After selection:

- the chosen event plays in full;
- a second event may leave a fragment;
- the third may be missed;
- other patrons later provide biased summaries.

Track:

- `witnessed_directly`;
- `heard_fragment`;
- `heard_secondhand_from`;
- `missed`.

Dialogue must not give the player direct-knowledge choices after only secondhand information.

---

## 8. Claim and contradiction model

A claim record contains:

- ID;
- speaker;
- topic;
- wording key;
- time made;
- public or private context;
- certainty expressed;
- evidence status;
- listeners present;
- contradiction links;
- whether Sasha may repeat it;
- social risk of repetition.

Example:

```yaml
id: claim_arkady_opposed_coup_from_start
speaker: arkady
subject: arkady
made_at: chapter_5
certainty: absolute
context: public
repeatable: true
contradicts:
  - claim_arkady_emergency_may_be_necessary
  - object_arkady_prepared_party_card
```

The interface should not label claims “true” or “false” unless the player has direct documentary support and the game is confident. It may show:

- “Arkady said…”
- “This conflicts with…”
- “You heard this from Lev, not directly.”

## 9. Memory-vignette examples

### 9.1 “The Signal” — Lev

The television rectangle expands into a transmission tower diagram. The bar stools become relay masts. Lev recalls a night of emergency repair. Arkady interrupts with the official version; one technician disappears from the composition. The player can ask whose name was removed.

Purpose:

- establishes memory as editable;
- connects broadcast technology to institutional control;
- reveals Lev's compromised action.

### 9.2 “The Number” — Gennady

Table chalk marks become a production board. Glasses become machine gauges. A red target number grows while an injured hand remains outside the frame. The player can focus on the number, the hand, or Arkady's signature.

Purpose:

- shows how abstract targets conceal bodies;
- ties worker and official together;
- avoids a literal factory location.

### 9.3 “Prague” — Nikolai

Counter shutters become armored silhouettes. Reflections in glass show civilians, but the central figure remains deliberately indistinct. Pavel narrates one version; Nikolai corrects him; the street layout shifts.

Purpose:

- makes uncertainty visible without treating responsibility as unknowable;
- links external intervention to troops in Moscow.

### 9.4 “The Blank Photograph” — Pavel and Sasha

A school group portrait appears across the wall. Names are written underneath, then one is painted out. Sasha's remembered classroom and Pavel's version disagree about whether he was afraid or dismissive.

Purpose:

- gives Sasha personal history;
- challenges Pavel's separation between ideal and institution.

## 10. Service choices

Serving is not a simple intoxication mechanic.

Possible actions:

- pour requested vodka;
- offer half measure;
- substitute tea;
- bring food first;
- say the bottle is finished;
- open a private bottle;
- clear a glass to end a conversation;
- leave a glass as permission to continue;
- settle or forgive a tab;
- ask for payment at a humiliating moment;
- give the telephone key or deny access.

Effects depend on person and context. A drink may increase disclosure, aggression, ritual comfort, nausea, or silence. No universal `alcohol = truth` rule.

---

## 11. Pacing

### Dialogue length

- normal line: 4–18 words;
- occasional longer line: 20–35 words;
- all lines over 35 words are rejected or explicitly reviewed;
- monologues broken by gestures, object actions, or interruptions;
- no patron speaks more than roughly 90 seconds without a player opportunity or visible change.

### Information density

Every historical fact must serve at least one dramatic function:

- change status;
- expose a contradiction;
- trigger a memory;
- alter a relationship;
- justify a practical choice;
- reframe a visible object.

Delete facts that exist only because research was interesting.

Treat _Papers, Please_ as a quality bar for compact human writing, ordinary work under pressure, tactile action, and consequences the player can read. Do not import its daily inspection structure, document-processing loop, cast patterns, choice framing, or narrative beats. Pressure here must come from tending this room, choosing attention, and living with what Sasha allows or misses.

At least 80 percent of meaningful slice choices should change an object, posture, availability, relationship, or attention result within the next two meaningful actions. Every major choice must also create a later cost or close another opportunity. Do not explain these changes with a morality meter or score tick.

### Quiet

After major reports, use 3–8 seconds of controlled silence, ambient sound, and posture change before choices appear. Do not fill every gap with score.

## 12. Ending assembly

The epilogue is generated from modules, not selected from one binary meter.

### Module groups

1. **Room state** — broken glass, burned paper, empty bottle, television left on/off, dawn light, unpaid tabs.
2. **Patron departures** — order, companions, destination stated or unknown.
3. **Preserved objects** — card, letter, photograph, notebook page, address book.
4. **Rewritten claims** — who reversed position and whether challenged.
5. **Sasha's ledger** — transactional, factual, interpretive, or blank.
6. **Galina's decision** — reopen normally, refuse a patron, sell/close, or continue without comment.

### Broad ending families

- **The Official Version:** the room converges on a clean retrospective account.
- **The Broken Table:** accusation and departure make reconciliation impossible.
- **The Private Archive:** Sasha preserves multiple conflicting records.
- **Nothing Written:** Sasha refuses to turn their confessions into ownership.
- **Morning Regulars:** the men return to routine despite everything.
- **The Last Shift:** Galina or Sasha closes the establishment's chapter.

Do not display these labels to the player.

## 13. Vertical slice outline

Recommended slice cast:

- Sasha;
- Galina;
- Arkady;
- Lev;
- Nikolai;
- Gennady.

Recommended slice chapters:

1. compressed “Everyone Is Winning”;
2. “The Dead at the Tunnel”;
3. compressed “The Sweater” and dawn epilogue.

Minimum authored content:

- 60 dialogue nodes;
- 24 ambient barks;
- 12 direct player questions;
- 12 challenge/repeat-claim choices;
- 8 service decisions;
- 5 silence continuations;
- 6 attention windows;
- 2 memory vignettes;
- 3 ending configurations.

Critical proof points:

- player misses meaningful content by choosing another focus;
- a patron changes his account after the broadcast;
- a service choice affects a confession but does not mechanically guarantee it;
- a contradiction can be confronted or preserved;
- the final ledger only contains witnessed knowledge.

---

## 14. Writing and review workflow

For each scene:

1. state the dramatic purpose in one sentence;
2. list public-event context available at that moment;
3. list what each present character wants in the scene;
4. identify the object or posture change;
5. draft the shortest functional exchange;
6. add choices and silence;
7. add conditions and consequences;
8. test with prior and later scenes;
9. remove exposition that does not change the room;
10. obtain historical and language review;
11. perform aloud;
12. enter content data only after the scene works as prose.

### Dialogue review questions

- Could this line be spoken by another patron unchanged? If yes, rewrite.
- Is the character explaining something solely for the player? If yes, motivate it.
- Does the choice reveal knowledge Sasha has not acquired? Remove or condition it.
- Does the scene move an object, relationship, posture, or belief? If no, cut or combine.
- Is the joke based on a victim or ethnicity? Remove it.
- Does every old man sound eloquent in the same way? Differentiate rhythm.
- Does a confession arrive because of a single drink? Add relational causality.

---

## 15. Narrative acceptance gate

The slice passes when:

- each principal patron is sympathetic in at least one concrete action and compromised in at least one concrete action;
- no character can be summarized accurately as only “the communist,” “the liberal,” “the soldier,” or “the worker”;
- silence changes at least five scenes;
- two playthroughs reveal different private truths;
- historical exposition never overwhelms present conflict;
- the public outcome remains fixed;
- the epilogue never invents knowledge the player missed;
- at least one player choice feels morally consequential without being scored as good or bad;
- the room's final emotional state reflects accumulated small actions.
- four of five blind playtesters can name the immediate tradeoff and one consequence after ten minutes without seeing hidden state.
